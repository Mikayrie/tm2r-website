Scabies

Scabies is an infestation of the skin by the human itch mite (*Sarcoptes scabiei* var. *hominis*). The microscopic mite burrows into the upper layer of the skin where it lives and lays its eggs. Scabies can spread quickly under crowded conditions where close body and skin contact is frequent.

Symptoms

The most common symptoms of scabies are intense itching (pruritus), especially at night, and a pimple-like skin rash. The itching and rash may affect much of the body or be limited to common sites such as:

  • Between the fingers
  • Wrist, elbow, or knee
  • Armpits and waist
  • Shoulder blades and buttocks

Causes and Risk Factors

Scabies is caused by an infestation of the human itch mite, *Sarcoptes scabiei*. It is usually spread by direct, prolonged, skin-to-skin contact with a person who has scabies. It can also be spread through infested items like bedding or clothes (World Health Organization, 2024).

  • Skin-to-Skin Contact: The most common mode of transmission.
  • Crowded Conditions: Outbreaks are common in nursing homes, prisons, and childcare facilities.
  • Weakened Immune System: People with compromised immune systems can develop a more severe form called crusted scabies.

Prevention and Management

Scabies is treatable. Management involves eliminating the infestation with prescribed medications and taking steps to prevent re-infestation.

  • Medication: Treatment is with topical creams (scabicides) or, in some cases, oral medication prescribed by a doctor.
  • Treating Contacts: All household members and close contacts should be treated at the same time, even if they don't show symptoms.
  • Decontamination: Bedding, clothing, and towels used by the infested person should be decontaminated by washing in hot water and drying on a hot cycle.
