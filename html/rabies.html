Rabies

Rabies is a vaccine-preventable viral disease which occurs in more than 150 countries and territories. It is a zoonosis, meaning it is transmitted from animals to humans. Dogs are the main source of human rabies deaths, contributing up to 99% of all rabies transmissions to humans. Once clinical symptoms appear, rabies is virtually 100% fatal.

Symptoms

The incubation period for rabies is typically 2â€“3 months but may vary. The initial symptoms of rabies include a fever with pain and unusual or unexplained tingling, pricking, or burning sensation at the wound site. As the virus spreads to the central nervous system, progressive and fatal inflammation of the brain and spinal cord develops.

  • Early symptoms: Fever, headache, nausea, vomiting.
  • Later symptoms can include anxiety, confusion, agitation, delirium, abnormal behavior, hallucinations, hydrophobia (fear of water), and insomnia.

Causes and Risk Factors

Rabies is caused by a Lyssavirus. The disease is transmitted to people and animals through saliva, usually via bites, scratches or direct contact with mucosa (e.g. eyes, mouth or nose). Children are at particular risk, as they tend to play with animals and may not report bites (World Health Organization, 2024).

  • Animal Bites: The vast majority of cases result from a bite from an infected dog.
  • Other Animals: Bats, raccoons, skunks, and foxes are other common carriers.
  • Risk Factors: Living in or travelling to rural areas where rabies is common and having contact with wild animals increases risk.

Prevention and Management

Rabies is a 100% vaccine-preventable disease. Preventing rabies in dogs is the most cost-effective strategy for preventing rabies in people. If an exposure occurs, prompt and effective treatment is critical.

  • Vaccinating Animals: Vaccinating dogs is the cornerstone of rabies prevention.
  • Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP): This is the immediate treatment of a bite victim after rabies exposure. This prevents virus entry into the central nervous system. It consists of extensive washing of the wound, administration of a potent and effective rabies vaccine, and injection of rabies immunoglobulin.
