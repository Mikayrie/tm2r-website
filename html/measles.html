Measles

Measles is a highly contagious disease caused by a virus. It spreads through the air when an infected person coughs or sneezes. It is a serious disease that can lead to severe complications and death. Before the introduction of the measles vaccine, major epidemics occurred approximately every 2â€“3 years.

Symptoms

The first sign of measles is usually a high fever, which begins about 10 to 12 days after exposure to the virus. Other early symptoms include:

  • A runny nose, a cough, and red and watery eyes.
  • Small white spots (Koplik spots) may develop inside the mouth.
  • After several days, a rash erupts, usually on the face and upper neck, before spreading down the body.

Causes and Risk Factors

Measles is caused by a virus from the paramyxovirus family. It is transmitted through direct contact with infectious droplets or by airborne spread when an infected person breathes, coughs, or sneezes (World Health Organization, 2024).

  • Airborne Transmission: The virus remains active and contagious in the air or on infected surfaces for up to 2 hours.
  • Risk Factors: Unvaccinated young children are at the highest risk. Any non-immune person (who has not been vaccinated or was vaccinated but did not develop immunity) can become infected.

Prevention and Management

Routine measles vaccination for children is the key public health strategy to reduce global measles deaths. There is no specific antiviral treatment for measles virus infection.

  • Vaccination: The measles vaccine is safe, effective, and inexpensive. WHO recommends 2 doses for all children.
  • Supportive Care: Severe complications can be avoided through supportive care that ensures good nutrition, adequate fluid intake and treatment of dehydration.
  • Vitamin A: All children diagnosed with measles should receive two doses of vitamin A supplements, given 24 hours apart.
