Conjunctivitis (Pink Eye)

Conjunctivitis, often called "pink eye," is an inflammation of the conjunctiva - the thin, clear tissue that lines the inside of the eyelid and covers the white part of the eye. It can be caused by viruses, bacteria, allergens, or irritants. Some forms are very contagious and can spread easily in schools and homes.

Symptoms

Symptoms of pink eye can vary depending on the cause but often include:

  • Pink or red color in the white of the eye(s)
  • Swelling of the conjunctiva and/or eyelids
  • Increased tear production
  • Itching, irritation, or burning sensation
  • Discharge (pus or mucus)
  • Crusting of eyelids or lashes, especially in the morning
  • Feeling like a foreign body is in the eye(s) or urge to rub the eye(s)
  • Contact lens discomfort or feeling that lenses don't stay in place

Note: Viral conjunctivitis often begins in one eye and may spread to the other, while allergic conjunctivitis usually affects both eyes.

Causes and Risk Factors

Pink eye has several different causes, each with distinct characteristics:

  • Viral Conjunctivitis: Caused by viruses associated with the common cold. Very contagious and can spread through respiratory droplets or contact with contaminated surfaces.
  • Bacterial Conjunctivitis: Caused by various bacteria. Can be serious and may cause more severe symptoms. Also contagious.
  • Allergic Conjunctivitis: Caused by eye allergens like pollen, pet dander, or dust mites. Not contagious and often seasonal.
  • Irritant Conjunctivitis: Caused by irritants like smoke, dust, chlorine in swimming pools, or foreign objects in the eye.

Risk Factors: Close contact with someone who has infectious conjunctivitis; exposure to allergens; using contaminated eye makeup or contact lenses; poor hand hygiene; recent upper respiratory infection.

Prevention and Management

Proper hygiene and care are essential to prevent the spread of contagious forms of pink eye and to manage symptoms:

  • Wash Hands Frequently: Use soap and warm water, especially after touching your eyes or face.
  • Avoid Touching Eyes: Try not to touch or rub your eyes with unwashed hands.
  • Don't Share Personal Items: Avoid sharing towels, washcloths, eye drops, makeup, contact lens cases, or glasses.
  • Proper Contact Lens Care: Follow your eye doctor's instructions for proper cleaning and don't wear contacts while infected.
  • Clean Surfaces: Disinfect countertops, doorknobs, and other frequently touched surfaces.
  • Management: Viral conjunctivitis usually clears on its own; bacterial may require antibiotic drops; allergic conjunctivitis can be treated with antihistamine drops. Cool compresses can help relieve discomfort.
  • When to See a Doctor: If you have moderate to severe pain, sensitivity to light, blurred vision, intense redness, symptoms that worsen or don't improve, or a weakened immune system.
