Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV)

HIV (human immunodeficiency virus) is a virus that attacks the bodyâ€™s immune system. If HIV is not treated, it can lead to AIDS (acquired immunodeficiency syndrome). There is currently no cure, but with proper medical care, HIV can be controlled. People with HIV who get effective treatment can live long, healthy lives and protect their partners.

Symptoms

The symptoms of HIV vary depending on the stage of infection. In the first few weeks after initial infection people may experience no symptoms or an influenza-like illness including:

  • Fever, headache, rash, or sore throat.
  • As the infection progresses, it weakens the immune system, and people can develop other signs like swollen lymph nodes, weight loss, and diarrhoea.
  • Without treatment, they could also develop severe illnesses such as tuberculosis, meningitis, and some cancers.

Causes and Risk Factors

HIV is spread through the exchange of a variety of body fluids from infected people, such as blood, breast milk, semen and vaginal secretions. Individuals cannot become infected through ordinary day-to-day contact like kissing, hugging, shaking hands, or sharing personal objects, food or water (World Health Organization, 2024).

  • Unprotected Sex: Having anal or vaginal sex without a condom.
  • Sharing Needles: Sharing contaminated needles, syringes and other injecting equipment.
  • Mother-to-child Transmission: HIV can be passed from a mother to her child during pregnancy, childbirth or breastfeeding.

Prevention and Management

There is no cure for HIV infection. However, effective antiretroviral (ARV) drugs can control the virus and help prevent transmission. Prevention involves avoiding risky behaviours.

  • Safer Sex: Using condoms correctly and consistently.
  • Testing and Counselling: Regular testing for HIV and other STIs.
  • Prevention of mother-to-child transmission (PMTCT): Antiretroviral drugs can significantly reduce the risk of transmission.
  • Antiretroviral therapy (ART): Daily use of ARV drugs can suppress the viral load to undetectable levels, which both keeps the person healthy and prevents sexual transmission.
