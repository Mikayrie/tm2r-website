Influenza (Seasonal)

Seasonal influenza (the flu) is an acute respiratory infection caused by influenza viruses which circulate in all parts of the world. For most people, the infection causes mild illness. However, influenza can cause severe illness or death, particularly in people at high risk, such as young children, older adults, and those with chronic health conditions.

Symptoms

Seasonal influenza is characterized by a sudden onset of symptoms. Most people recover within a week without requiring medical attention. Common symptoms include:

  • Fever, cough (usually dry), and a sore throat.
  • Headache, muscle and joint pain.
  • Runny nose and severe malaise (feeling unwell).
  • The cough can be severe and can last 2 or more weeks.

Causes and Risk Factors

Seasonal influenza viruses spread easily, with rapid transmission in crowded areas. When an infected person coughs or sneezes, droplets containing viruses are dispersed into the air and can infect persons in close proximity (World Health Organization, 2023).

  • Airborne Droplets: Inhaling droplets from an infected person's cough or sneeze.
  • Contaminated Surfaces: Touching surfaces contaminated with the virus and then touching one's own mouth or nose.
  • Risk Groups: High-risk groups include pregnant women, children under 5, the elderly, individuals with chronic medical conditions, and healthcare workers.

Prevention and Management

The most effective way to prevent the disease is vaccination. Management focuses on relieving symptoms. People with uncomplicated flu should rest and drink plenty of fluids.

  • Vaccination: Annual vaccination is recommended, especially for high-risk groups.
  • Hygiene: Regular hand washing and covering coughs and sneezes can help limit spread.
  • Isolation: People who are sick should stay home to avoid infecting others.
  • Antiviral Drugs: Antiviral medications can reduce the severity and duration of illness but need to be prescribed by a doctor.
