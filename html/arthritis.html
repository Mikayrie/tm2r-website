Rheumatoid Arthritis

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is a chronic autoimmune disease that primarily causes inflammation, pain, swelling, and stiffness in the joints. It typically affects the hands, feet and knees. In RA, the body's immune system mistakenly attacks healthy cells in the joints, leading to damage and disability if not treated.

Symptoms

The main symptoms of rheumatoid arthritis are joint pain, swelling, and stiffness. It may also cause more general symptoms and inflammation in other parts of the body.

  • Tender, warm, swollen joints, often affecting smaller joints first.
  • Joint stiffness that is usually worse in the mornings and after inactivity.
  • Fatigue, fever and loss of appetite.
  • Symptoms often affect the same joints on both sides of the body (symmetrically).

  • Genetic Susceptibility: Some people may inherit genes that make them more likely to develop RA.
  • Environmental Triggers: Factors such as smoking and certain infections may trigger the disease in genetically susceptible people.
  • Hormonal Factors: RA is more common in women than men, suggesting hormones may play a role.

Prevention and Management

There is no cure for RA, but early diagnosis and treatment can control the disease and prevent joint damage and disability. Management includes medications, self-management strategies, and lifestyle changes.

  • Medications: Disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs (DMARDs) and biologics can slow the disease and prevent joint deformity.
  • Physical Therapy: Exercises can help keep joints flexible and strengthen muscles.
  • Lifestyle: Regular physical activity, maintaining a healthy weight, and quitting smoking can help manage the condition.
