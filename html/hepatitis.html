Hepatitis

Hepatitis is an inflammation of the liver that can cause a range of health problems and can be fatal. There are five main strains of the hepatitis virus, referred to as types A, B, C, D and E. These five types are of greatest concern because of the burden of illness and death they cause and the potential for outbreaks and epidemic spread.

Symptoms

Many people with hepatitis do not have symptoms. For those that do, acute symptoms can include:

  • Fever, fatigue, and loss of appetite.
  • Nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain.
  • Dark urine, pale stools, joint pain, and jaundice (yellowing of skin and eyes).
  • Chronic infection with hepatitis B or C can lead to cirrhosis or liver cancer.

Causes and Risk Factors

The different hepatitis viruses have different modes of transmission. Hepatitis A and E are typically caused by ingestion of contaminated food or water. Hepatitis B, C and D usually occur as a result of parenteral contact with infected body fluids (World Health Organization, 2024).

  • Hepatitis A/E: Ingesting contaminated food or water.
  • Hepatitis B/C/D: Contact with infected blood (e.g., through unsafe injections or transfusions), sexual contact, or from an infected mother to her baby.
  • Risk Factors: Include unsafe injections, unprotected sex, and living in areas with poor sanitation.

Prevention and Management

Safe and effective vaccines are available to prevent hepatitis A, B, and E. Treatment options exist for chronic hepatitis B and C, which can control the virus and prevent severe liver damage.

  • Vaccination: Vaccines are a key tool in preventing hepatitis A and B.
  • Safe Practices: Ensuring blood safety, safe injection practices, and safer sex help prevent B and C.
  • Food and Water Safety: Improved sanitation and food safety are the most effective ways to combat hepatitis A and E.
  • Antiviral Medications: Effective medicines are available to treat chronic hepatitis B and cure most cases of hepatitis C.
