Diarrhoeal Disease

Diarrhoea is defined as the passage of three or more loose or liquid stools per day. It is a symptom of an infection in the intestinal tract, which can be caused by a variety of bacterial, viral and parasitic organisms. Diarrhoea is a leading cause of malnutrition in children under five years old, and severe diarrhoea can lead to dehydration and death.

Symptoms

The main symptom is the passage of loose, watery stools three or more times a day. Other symptoms associated with diarrhoea may include:

  • Abdominal cramps or pain.
  • Fever and nausea.
  • Dehydration, indicated by thirst, dry mouth, little or no urination, and weakness.
  • Bloody stools (in cases of dysentery).

Causes and Risk Factors

Diarrhoea is a symptom of infections caused by a host of bacterial, viral and parasitic organisms, most of which are spread by faecally-contaminated water. Infection is more common when there is a shortage of adequate sanitation and hygiene and safe water for drinking, cooking and cleaning (World Health Organization, 2022).

  • Infection: Viruses such as rotavirus and norovirus, and bacteria such as E. coli and Salmonella.
  • Contaminated Food and Water: The main route of transmission.
  • Malnutrition: Malnourished children are more vulnerable to diarrhoea.
  • Poor Hygiene: Lack of handwashing can easily spread infectious agents.

Prevention and Management

Key measures to prevent diarrhoeal disease include access to safe drinking-water, use of improved sanitation, and hand washing with soap. Management focuses on rehydration to prevent death from dehydration.

  • Rehydration: Using oral rehydration salts (ORS) solution to replace lost fluids and electrolytes.
  • Zinc Supplements: Zinc supplements reduce the duration of a diarrhoea episode by 25%.
  • Nutrient-rich foods: Continued feeding during diarrhoea helps a child's recovery.
  • Vaccination: Rotavirus vaccination is an effective preventive measure for children.
