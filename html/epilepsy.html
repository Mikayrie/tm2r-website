Epilepsy

Epilepsy is a chronic noncommunicable disease of the brain that affects people of all ages. It is characterized by recurrent seizures, which are brief episodes of involuntary movement that may involve a part of the body (partial) or the entire body (generalized) and are sometimes accompanied by loss of consciousness and control of bowel or bladder function.

Symptoms

Seizures are the main sign of epilepsy. Seizure characteristics vary and depend on where in the brain the disturbance first starts, and how far it spreads. Temporary symptoms occur, such as:

  • Loss of awareness or consciousness.
  • Disturbances of movement, sensation (including vision, hearing and taste), mood, or other cognitive functions.
  • Involuntary jerking movements of the arms and legs (convulsions).
  • A staring spell or temporary confusion.

Causes and Risk Factors

The cause of the disease is still unknown in about half of the cases globally. In other cases, the causes can be various factors, including brain damage from prenatal causes, stroke, brain tumors, or severe head injury. Some types of epilepsy are linked to genetic factors (World Health Organization, 2024).

  • Structural: A stroke or brain tumour can lead to epilepsy.
  • Genetic: Some epilepsies run in families, caused by a change in a gene.
  • Infectious: Infections of the brain such as meningitis or neurocysticercosis can cause epilepsy.
  • Metabolic and Immune causes.

Prevention and Management

Epilepsy is treatable. It is possible to control seizures in up to 70% of people with epilepsy with anti-seizure medicines. Prevention focuses on avoiding known risk factors.

  • Preventing Head Injury: Wearing helmets and seatbelts can reduce the risk of epilepsy from head trauma.
  • Perinatal Care: Adequate care before and during birth can reduce new cases of epilepsy caused by birth injury.
  • Medication: Daily use of anti-seizure medicines can successfully control seizures.
  • Lifestyle: Getting enough sleep and avoiding seizure triggers (like stress or flashing lights for some people) can help manage the condition.
