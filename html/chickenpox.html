Chickenpox (Varicella)

Chickenpox is an acute and highly contagious disease caused by the varicella-zoster virus (VZV). It results in a characteristic itchy rash with small, fluid-filled blisters. While it is most common in children, adults can also contract the disease, often with more severe symptoms.

Symptoms

The classic sign of chickenpox is a rash that turns into itchy, fluid-filled blisters that eventually turn into scabs. Other typical symptoms that may appear one to two days before the rash include:

  • Fever
  • Tiredness and a general feeling of being unwell (malaise)
  • Loss of appetite
  • Headache

Causes and Risk Factors

Chickenpox is caused by the varicella-zoster virus (VZV). It is extremely contagious and spreads easily through the air when an infected person coughs or sneezes, or through direct contact with fluid from the chickenpox blisters (World Health Organization, 2022).

  • Direct Contact: Touching the blisters of an infected person.
  • Airborne Transmission: Inhaling virus particles from an infected person's cough or sneeze.
  • Risk Factors: The main risk factor is not having had the disease before or not being vaccinated against it.

Prevention and Management

The most effective way to prevent chickenpox is through vaccination. Management of the disease focuses on relieving symptoms while the body fights off the infection.

  • Vaccination: The varicella vaccine is safe and effective at preventing the disease.
  • Symptom Relief: Calamine lotion and cool baths can help relieve itching. Over-the-counter medications can be used to reduce fever.
  • Isolation: Staying home and avoiding contact with others is crucial to prevent the spread of the virus.
