Gastroenteritis

Gastroenteritis, often called the "stomach flu," is an inflammation of the stomach and intestines that typically results in acute diarrhea. It can be caused by viruses, bacteria, parasites, or other microorganisms. While usually not serious in healthy adults, viral gastroenteritis can cause severe dehydration and be life-threatening for infants, older adults, and people with compromised immune systems.

Symptoms

Gastroenteritis symptoms can range from mild to severe and typically appear within one to three days after exposure. The main symptoms usually last for a day or two but can occasionally persist for up to 10 days.

  • Watery, usually nonbloody diarrhea
  • Nausea, vomiting or both
  • Stomach cramps and pain
  • Occasional muscle aches or headache
  • Low-grade fever

Causes and Risk Factors

Gastroenteritis can be caused by several different viruses, bacteria, or parasites. The most common cause is viral infection, particularly norovirus and rotavirus.

  • Viral Causes: Norovirus, rotavirus, adenovirus, and astrovirus
  • Bacterial Causes: E. coli, salmonella, campylobacter, and shigella
  • Parasitic Causes: Giardia lamblia and cryptosporidium
  • Risk Factors: Young children, older adults, people with weakened immune systems, and those living in or traveling to areas with poor sanitation

Prevention and Management

There's no specific treatment for viral gastroenteritis, so prevention is key. Antibiotics aren't effective against viruses, and overusing them can contribute to antibiotic-resistant bacteria.

  • Hydration: The most important treatment is replacing lost fluids and electrolytes
  • Hand Hygiene: Wash hands thoroughly with soap and water, especially after using the toilet
  • Food Safety: Avoid contaminated food and water, and practice proper food handling
  • Surface Disinfection: Clean contaminated surfaces thoroughly
  • Vaccination: Rotavirus vaccine is available for infants
