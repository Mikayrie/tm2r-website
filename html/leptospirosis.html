Leptospirosis

Leptospirosis is a bacterial disease that affects humans and animals. It is caused by bacteria of the genus Leptospira. The bacteria are spread through the urine of infected animals, which can get into water or soil and can survive there for weeks to months. The disease occurs worldwide but is most common in tropical and subtropical areas with high rainfall.

Symptoms

Leptospirosis can cause a wide range of symptoms. Some infected persons may have no symptoms at all. Common symptoms include:

  • High fever and chills.
  • Headache and muscle aches.
  • Vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain.
  • Jaundice (yellow skin and eyes), red eyes, and rash.

Without treatment, Leptospirosis can lead to kidney damage, meningitis, liver failure, respiratory distress, and even death.

Causes and Risk Factors

Humans can become infected through contact with urine (or other body fluids, except saliva) from infected animals, or contact with water, soil, or food contaminated with the urine of infected animals. The disease is not known to be spread from person to person.

Prevention and Management

Leptospirosis is treated with antibiotics. Prevention strategies must be based on local conditions and should target the sources of infection. Key prevention methods include:

  • Wearing protective clothing for those with occupational exposure.
  • Avoiding swimming in water that may be contaminated.
  • Controlling the rodent population.
