Depression

Depression is a common mental disorder. It is characterized by persistent sadness and a loss of interest or pleasure in previously rewarding or enjoyable activities. It can also disturb sleep and appetite. It is different from usual mood fluctuations and short-lived emotional responses to challenges in everyday life.

Symptoms

During a depressive episode, a person can experience a depressed mood (feeling sad, irritable, empty) or a loss of pleasure or interest in activities, for most of the day, nearly every day, for at least two weeks. Other symptoms include:

  • Poor concentration and feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt.
  • Hopelessness about the future.
  • Disturbed sleep or changes in appetite or weight.
  • Feeling very tired or low in energy.

Causes and Risk Factors

Depression results from a complex interaction of social, psychological and biological factors. People who have gone through adverse life events (unemployment, bereavement, traumatic events) are more likely to develop depression (World Health Organization, 2023).

  • Life Events: Adversity in childhood, loss, and unemployment can contribute to depression.
  • Genetics: Having a family history of depression increases the risk.
  • Biological Factors: Imbalances in brain chemicals (neurotransmitters) are thought to play a role.
  • Chronic Illness: The stress of living with a serious illness can trigger depression.

Prevention and Management

There are effective psychological treatments and medications for depression. Prevention programs have been shown to reduce the incidence of depression.

  • Psychological Treatments: Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) and interpersonal psychotherapy (IPT) are effective.
  • Medications: Antidepressants, such as selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) are commonly prescribed.
  • Self-Care: Regular exercise, adequate sleep, and a healthy diet can help. Staying connected with people and engaging in enjoyable activities is also important.
