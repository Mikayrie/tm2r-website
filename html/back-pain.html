Low Back Pain

Low back pain is a very common health problem worldwide and a major cause of disability, affecting performance at work and general well-being. It can be experienced as a dull ache or a sharp, shooting pain. It can be acute (lasting less than 6 weeks), sub-acute (6â€“12 weeks) or chronic (more than 12 weeks).

Symptoms

The main symptom of low back pain is an ache or pain anywhere on the back, and sometimes all the way down to the buttocks and legs. Some back issues can cause pain in other parts of the body, depending on the nerves affected.

  • Muscle ache in the lower back.
  • Shooting or stabbing pain.
  • Pain that radiates down your leg.
  • Pain that worsens with bending, lifting, standing or walking.

Causes and Risk Factors

Most low back pain is non-specific, meaning there is no identifiable disease or structural reason for the pain. Factors associated with low back pain are both individual and occupational (World Health Organization, 2023).

  • Individual Factors: Older age, low levels of physical activity, obesity, and smoking.
  • Occupational Factors: Jobs that involve heavy lifting, forceful movements, or vibration.
  • Psychological Factors: Stress, anxiety, and depression are associated with low back pain.

Prevention and Management

Management for low back pain depends on the nature of the pain. For non-specific pain, self-care and staying active are key. Prevention involves maintaining a healthy lifestyle.

  • Stay Active: Regular physical activity helps to strengthen back muscles and improve posture.
  • Self-Care: For acute pain, options include heat packs and staying active.
  • Psychological Therapies: For chronic pain, cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) and other therapies can be helpful.
  • Maintain a Healthy Weight: Excess weight puts added stress on the back.
