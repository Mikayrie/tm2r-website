Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is a condition where the kidneys are damaged and cannot filter blood as well as they should. Because of this, excess fluid and waste from blood remain in the body and may cause other health problems. CKD is a major cause of catastrophic health expenditure and can lead to kidney failure, requiring dialysis or a kidney transplant to survive.

Symptoms

Most people may not have any severe symptoms until their kidney disease is advanced. However, some signs might include:

  • Feeling more tired and having less energy.
  • Trouble concentrating and poor appetite.
  • Swollen feet and ankles.
  • Puffiness around the eyes, especially in the morning, and urinating more often.

Causes and Risk Factors

The most common causes of CKD are diabetes and high blood pressure, which can damage the small blood vessels in the kidneys. Some kidney conditions are inherited, or may be caused by drugs and toxins (World Health Organization, 2021).

  • Diabetes: The leading cause of kidney failure.
  • High Blood Pressure (Hypertension): Can damage blood vessels in the kidneys over time.
  • Other Conditions: Glomerulonephritis (inflammation of the kidney's filtering units), inherited diseases like polycystic kidney disease, and recurrent kidney infections.

Prevention and Management

Early detection and treatment can often keep chronic kidney disease from getting worse. Management focuses on slowing the progression of the disease and controlling underlying causes.

  • Managing Blood Pressure: Keeping blood pressure within a target range is crucial.
  • Controlling Blood Sugar: For people with diabetes, good glucose control can slow the progression of kidney disease.
    • -
  • Medication: Medications like ACE inhibitors and ARBs can protect the kidneys.
  • Lifestyle: A healthy diet low in sodium, regular exercise, and avoiding smoking are important.
  • Treatment for Kidney Failure: Options include dialysis or a kidney transplant.
