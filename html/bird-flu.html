Chickenpox (Varicella)

Chickenpox is a highly contagious disease caused by the varicella-zoster virus (VZV). It causes an itchy, blister-like rash that appears first on the chest, back, and face, and then spreads over the entire body. While chickenpox was once very common, the introduction of the chickenpox vaccine has significantly reduced the number of cases.

Symptoms

Chickenpox symptoms typically appear 10 to 21 days after exposure to the virus and last about 5 to 10 days. The most recognizable symptom is an itchy rash that turns into fluid-filled blisters that eventually become scabs. Other symptoms may include fever, headache, loss of appetite, and tiredness. The rash goes through three phases: raised pink or red bumps (papules), small fluid-filled blisters (vesicles), and crusts and scabs that cover the broken blisters.

Causes and Risk Factors

Chickenpox is caused by the varicella-zoster virus. It can spread through direct contact with the rash or through droplets in the air when an infected person coughs or sneezes. You're at increased risk of contracting chickenpox if you haven't already had it, haven't been vaccinated, work in or attend a school or child care facility, or live with children. Most people who have had chickenpox or have been vaccinated are immune.

Prevention and Management

The chickenpox (varicella) vaccine is the best way to prevent chickenpox. For those who get chickenpox, management focuses on relieving symptoms while the body fights the virus. This includes using calamine lotion and colloidal oatmeal baths to relieve itching, acetaminophen to reduce fever, and avoiding scratching to prevent skin infections and scarring. Antiviral medications may be prescribed for people at high risk of complications.
