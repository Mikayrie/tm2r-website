Mononucleosis (Epstein-Barr Virus)

Mononucleosis, commonly called "mono," is a contagious illness typically caused by the Epstein-Barr virus (EBV). It's often called the "kissing disease" because it spreads through saliva, but you can also get it through other close contact. Mono is most common in teenagers and young adults, though anyone can get it.

Symptoms

Symptoms of mononucleosis can range from mild to severe and typically appear four to six weeks after infection. They usually last for one to two months but can persist longer in some cases.

  • Extreme fatigue and weakness that may last for weeks
  • Sore throat that is often severe and doesn't improve with antibiotics
  • Fever and chills
  • Swollen lymph nodes in the neck and armpits
  • Headaches and body aches
  • Swollen tonsils with white patches
  • Skin rash, especially if taking certain antibiotics
  • Swollen spleen or liver (less common)

Causes and Risk Factors

The Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) is the primary cause of mononucleosis, though other viruses can sometimes cause similar symptoms. EBV is one of the most common human viruses worldwide.

  • Transmission: Primarily through saliva - kissing, sharing drinks, utensils, or toothbrushes. It can also spread through coughing, sneezing, or contact with other bodily fluids.
  • Age: Teenagers and young adults (15-24 years) are most likely to develop noticeable symptoms.
  • Weakened Immune System: People with compromised immune systems are at higher risk.
  • Close Contact Settings: Students, military personnel, and healthcare workers have increased exposure risk.

Prevention and Management

There is no vaccine for mononucleosis. Prevention focuses on avoiding exposure to infected saliva and practicing good hygiene. Treatment aims to relieve symptoms while your body fights the infection.

  • Avoid Sharing: Don't share drinks, food, utensils, or personal items with someone who has mono.
  • Rest: Get plenty of rest to help your body fight the infection.
  • Hydration: Drink plenty of fluids to prevent dehydration.
  • Pain Relief: Use over-the-counter pain relievers like acetaminophen or ibuprofen for fever and discomfort.
  • Sore Throat Care: Gargle with salt water, use throat lozenges, or drink warm liquids.
  • Avoid Physical Activity: Refrain from contact sports and heavy lifting for at least 3-4 weeks to prevent spleen rupture.
